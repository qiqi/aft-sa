\begin{abstract}
We present a single-equation Reynolds-averaged Navier--Stokes (RANS) transition
model in which the Spalart--Allmaras (SA) working variable $\tilde\nu$ serves as
its own transition indicator. Since $\tilde\nu$ already spans laminar to fully
turbulent values, we repurpose its sub-$O(\nu)$ range---inert in the baseline
model---to carry a Tollmien--Schlichting amplification factor, so that a single
modified production term drives the one SA transport equation, unchanged in
structure, through laminar instability growth and turbulent eddy-viscosity
production, handing over near $\chi\!=\!\tilde\nu/\nu\!\sim\!1$. No auxiliary
field is introduced---in contrast to conventional RANS transition models, which
add a transport equation for an auxiliary variable (amplification factor,
intermittency, or transition Reynolds number) or gate turbulent production with
an algebraic intermittency. The amplification rate depends only on the local mean flow---a
vorticity Reynolds number and a profile-fullness indicator---so the production
retains an analytic Jacobian and integrates implicitly at the cost of the
baseline model. The amplification kernel is anchored to canonical stability
limits---the free-shear rate, the Blasius $e^N$ envelope, and the Falkner--Skan
pressure-gradient response---and then exercised across three pressure-gradient
regimes of increasing complexity. On a zero-pressure-gradient flat plate the
single freestream-turbulence closure---Mack's $e^N$ critical-$N$-factor
correlation, adopted rather than fit---places transition onset within $\sim\!8\%$
of the Abu-Ghannam \& Shaw correlation across the natural-transition turbulence
range, verifying the Blasius envelope. On the NLF(1)-0416
natural-laminar-flow airfoil at $Re\!=\!4\!\times\!10^6$ one
favorable-gradient onset-delay constant is set so the onset of growth matches
the $e^N$ rooftop ignition, and the model then predicts the laminar-to-turbulent
handover on both surfaces across $\alpha\!\in\!\{0^\circ,4^\circ,9^\circ,15^\circ\}$
and across three refinement levels of two independently-generated mesh families
(twenty-four cases). On the Eppler 387 at $Re\!=\!2\!\times\!10^5$ the
adverse-pressure-gradient behavior---with no further constant---is predicted in
the low-Reynolds laminar-separation-bubble regime; the signed skin friction shows the
recirculation directly and the transition tracks the same $e^N$ reference at
the canonical incidences $\alpha\!\in\!\{0^\circ,2^\circ,5^\circ,7^\circ\}$
across another twenty-four cases on the same two-family ladder. The three
stages each target a distinct pressure-gradient regime --- zero, favorable,
and adverse --- and reuse the same constants throughout.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Laminar flow over a lifting surface offers among the largest viscous-drag
reductions available, and predicting where it ends is a first-order requirement
for natural-laminar-flow design.
In a quiet low-disturbance environment the laminar boundary layer first becomes
unstable to gentle two-dimensional Tollmien--Schlichting (TS) waves
\cite{mack_1977,schmid_henningson_2001}: as the layer thickens, the envelope of the
strongest TS mode grows quasi-exponentially over a long streamwise stretch until,
at an amplitude of a few percent of the mean, three-dimensional secondary
instabilities trigger localized turbulent spots that spread and merge into a fully
turbulent layer \cite{schubauer_klebanoff_1956,schmid_henningson_2001}. The slow
TS-envelope stage occupies most of the streamwise distance and largely determines
where transition is observed, so it is the stage a transition model must reproduce.
The classical tool is the $e^N$ method \cite{smith_gamberoni_1956, vaningen_2008}:
linear stability theory is integrated along the boundary layer to accumulate an
amplification factor $N$, and transition is declared where $N$ reaches an empirical
threshold $N_\mathrm{crit}$ set by the disturbance environment \cite{mack_1977}.
Drela and Giles reduced the stability integration to an approximate envelope
correlation, making $e^N$ practical inside an integral-boundary-layer method
\cite{drela_giles_1987, drela_xfoil_1989}.

The $e^N$ method is non-local: it presumes a boundary layer that can be marched and
integrated, which is foreign to a general unstructured RANS solver. The community
has therefore recast transition in terms of locally computable transport equations,
along three lines. \emph{Amplification factor transport} carries $N$ itself: Coder
and Maughmer's AFT model transports an amplification factor whose source reproduces
the $e^N$ envelope and couples it to SA through the $f_{t2}$ term, the variant
catalogued as SA-AFT2017b on the NASA Turbulence Modeling Resource adding a second
(intermittency) equation \cite{coder_maughmer_2014, nasa_tmr}; Halila et al.
subsequently smoothed this model (AFT-S) for implicit Newton--Krylov solution in
gradient-based optimization, retaining the separate amplification-factor equation
\cite{halila_2022}. \emph{Local-correlation intermittency transport} instead carries
an intermittency: the Langtry--Menter $\gamma$--$Re_\theta$ model adds two transport
equations \cite{menter_langtry_2006, langtry_menter_2009}, reduced to one in a later
$\gamma$-only formulation \cite{menter_gamma_2015} and coupled to SA by Medida and
Baeder \cite{medida_baeder_2011}; Walters and Cokljat instead transport a
laminar-fluctuation energy \cite{walters_cokljat_2008}. Finally, \emph{algebraic}
models add no transport equation at all: the Bas--Cakmakcioglu model and its
revision (BC/BCM) multiply the SA production by an intermittency computed from
purely local correlations \cite{cakmakcioglu_2018, cakmakcioglu_2020}, an approach
extended in several recent SA modifications \cite{rahman_2024, crivellini_2023}.

These nearest neighbors share a common premise that we depart from: the SA working
variable $\tilde\nu$ is treated as a purely turbulent quantity, switched on either
by an extra transported field (AFT, $\gamma$--$Re_\theta$) or by an external
algebraic intermittency (BC/BCM). Equation count alone therefore does not separate
the present approach from the algebraic family, which is also single-equation. The
distinction is in \emph{what the field represents}. A scaling argument shows the
sub-$O(1)$ range of $\tilde\nu$ is the natural home for a TS-envelope variable. The SA working variable carries the dimensions of an eddy viscosity, i.e.\
a product $u'\ell$ of a fluctuation-velocity scale and a length scale. In a
Blasius-like layer just before secondary instability, the TS-envelope velocity
amplitude $u'$ is a few percent of the mean and the wall-normal extent $\ell$
over which that fluctuation is coherent is a fraction of the
boundary-layer thickness at
$Re_\theta\!\sim\!\mathcal{O}(10^2)$--$\mathcal{O}(10^3)$
\cite{schmid_henningson_2001,abu_ghannam_shaw_1980}; their product $u'\ell$ is at
most a few times the laminar viscosity $\nu$. Assigning that product to the same
working variable, $\tilde\nu\!\sim\!u'\ell$, makes $\chi=\tilde\nu/\nu$ at most
$\mathcal{O}(1)$ in the pre-transitional layer.
The region $\chi\!\lesssim\!\mathcal{O}(1)$ is exactly the part of the SA state space
that contributes negligibly to the eddy viscosity and is largely inert in the
baseline model \cite{spalart_allmaras_1994}. The natural amplitude and length scales
of laminar instabilities therefore correspond to a range of $\tilde\nu$ that SA
barely uses---a coincidence we exploit: we let that range \emph{be} the amplification
factor. In the laminar regime the production of $\tilde\nu$ is replaced by a local,
envelope-method-inspired instability rate, so $\ln\tilde\nu$ accumulates
monotonically in the manner of an amplification factor; as $\tilde\nu$ approaches its
turbulent level the production reverts smoothly to the standard SA form. One
transport equation---the SA equation, unchanged in structure---spans laminar
instability and turbulence with no auxiliary field. We refer to the model as
SA-AI\footnote{Where the prevailing pursuit of artificial intelligence is all
Yang---ever more parameters, more compute---this model is its Yin: transition
arises autogenously, from a variable the baseline equation already carries, at
essentially no added cost. Here ``AI'' abbreviates \emph{Autogenous Inception},
not the other kind.} (Spalart--Allmaras with \emph{A}utogenous
\emph{I}nception): the SA working variable generates its own transition from
within, with no added field. It is distinct from Coder's SA-AFT, in which a
separate amplification factor is transported.

The contribution is thus a model that occupies an otherwise empty cell
(Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}): amplification-factor ($e^N$-like) physics carried by the
turbulence working variable itself, with no auxiliary equation and a local,
analytically differentiable source.

\section{Model Formulation}\label{sec:model}

The Spalart--Allmaras model \cite{spalart_allmaras_1992} solves a transport equation
for $\tilde\nu$,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{D\tilde\nu}{Dt} = P_\mathrm{SA} - D_\mathrm{SA}
  + \frac{1}{\sigma}\nabla\!\cdot\!\big[(\nu+\tilde\nu)\nabla\tilde\nu\big]
  + \frac{c_{b2}}{\sigma}|\nabla\tilde\nu|^2,
  \qquad
  P_\mathrm{SA} = c_{b1}\tilde S\,\tilde\nu,
  \quad
  D_\mathrm{SA} = c_{w1}f_w\!\left(\frac{\tilde\nu}{d}\right)^{\!2},
  \label{eq:sa}
\end{equation}
in the standard form of Ref.~\cite{spalart_allmaras_1992}, with the modified
vorticity $\tilde S$, the wall function $f_w$, the constants $c_{b1},c_{b2},c_{w1},
\sigma$, and the eddy viscosity $\mu_t=\rho\,\tilde\nu f_{v1}(\chi)$,
$\chi=\tilde\nu/\nu$, all as defined there; we use the negative-$\tilde\nu$
safeguards of the standard implementation. The eddy-viscosity function
$f_{v1}(\chi)\!=\!\chi^3/(\chi^3+c_{v1}^3)$ vanishes cubically for
$\chi\!\ll\!c_{v1}$, $f_{v1}\!\approx\!(\chi/c_{v1})^3\!=\!\chi^3/357.9\!\approx\!
2.8\!\times\!10^{-3}\,\chi^3$ (with $c_{v1}\!=\!7.1$): even at $\chi\!=\!1$ the
eddy viscosity $\mu_t\!=\!\rho\,\tilde\nu f_{v1}$ is only $\sim\!0.3\%$ of
$\rho\tilde\nu$, and it falls off as $\chi^3$ below that. The field $\tilde\nu$
therefore contributes negligibly to the mean flow while $\chi\lesssim1$: this
sub-$O(1)$ range is dynamically inert in the baseline model and is what we
repurpose.

In the laminar range we replace the turbulent production $P_\mathrm{SA}$ by a
laminar amplification production of the same algebraic form,
\begin{equation}
  P_\mathrm{AFT} = a(Re_\Omega,\Gamma,\lambda_p)\,\omega\,\tilde\nu ,
  \label{eq:paft}
\end{equation}
where $\omega$ is the vorticity magnitude and $a$ is a nondimensional
amplification rate built from local flow indicators (defined below). The form
mirrors $P_\mathrm{SA}=c_{b1}\tilde S\,\tilde\nu$: the vorticity magnitude
$\omega$ (units $T^{-1}$) supplies the inverse time scale in place of
$c_{b1}\tilde S$, so with $a$ dimensionless the laminar production carries the
same dimensions as the turbulent one, $[\tilde\nu]/[t]=L^2T^{-3}$. The remainder
of this section constructs $a$ and the laminar$\to$turbulent handover.

\subsection{Laminar amplification production}
The rate $a$ is built in the spirit of approximate-envelope $e^N$ methods. The
envelope $e^N$ rate $dN/dRe_\theta$ is naturally written in terms of the integral
momentum thickness $\theta$, but $\theta$ is awkward to evaluate in a general RANS
field because it requires integration ``to the edge'' of a boundary layer whose edge
may be ill-defined under shear-layer merging, separation, or freestream turbulence.
A clean local surrogate is the vorticity Reynolds number
$Re_\Omega=d^2\omega/\nu$ \cite{VanDriest1963, MenterLangtry2006, langtry_menter_2009}:
in a Blasius boundary layer the peak of $Re_\Omega$ inside the BL profile is
proportional to $Re_\theta$ ($\max_y Re_\Omega\!\approx\!2.2\,Re_\theta$) and is
located at $y\!\approx\!\theta$, so $Re_\Omega$ inherits the role of $Re_\theta$
without the nonlocal integration. We adopt $Re_\Omega$ as the
amplification indicator and pair it with two purely local descriptors of the
boundary-layer state,
\begin{equation}
  Re_\Omega = \frac{d^2\,\omega}{\nu}, \qquad
  \lambda_p = -\,\frac{d^{2}}{\rho\,\nu\,|\mathbf{u}|^{2}}\,\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla p, \qquad
  \Gamma = \frac{2(\omega d)^2}{|\mathbf{u}|^2+(\omega d)^2}.
  \label{eq:indicators}
\end{equation}

The first of these, $\lambda_p$, is a local pressure-gradient parameter of the
Pohlhausen/Thwaites type---the same quantity that enters the Abu-Ghannam \& Shaw
transition-onset correlation through the Thwaites parameter
\cite{abu_ghannam_shaw_1980}. It is positive in a favorable gradient
(accelerating flow, $\partial p/\partial s\!<\!0$), zero on a
zero-pressure-gradient (Blasius) profile, and negative in an adverse gradient.
Because it reads the \emph{instantaneous, local} pressure gradient, $\lambda_p$
is an excellent sensor for the favorable-gradient regions where amplification
must be held off (it drives the suppression term introduced below). What it
cannot do is record \emph{history}: an adverse gradient destabilizes a layer
\emph{cumulatively}---the integrated adverse loading fills out the velocity
profile and raises the shape factor $H$ downstream---whereas a pointwise
$\partial p/\partial s$ carries no memory of that accumulation. A profile already
driven toward separation by an upstream adverse run can sit at a small local
$\lambda_p$ yet be strongly unstable.

The second descriptor, $\Gamma\in[0,2]$, supplies what $\lambda_p$ misses: it
measures the \emph{fullness} of the local velocity profile. It approaches unity
in an attached near-wall shear layer (where $\omega d\!\sim\!|\mathbf{u}|$) and
grows toward $2$ on an inflectional, near-separation profile, so it reflects the
accumulated state of the layer rather than the local gradient---a local
surrogate for the role the shape factor plays in envelope methods, though not
$H$ itself. $\Gamma$ is built only from $\omega$, $d$, and the flow speed
$|\mathbf{u}|$ measured relative to the nearest wall point; with that body-fixed
reference all three quantities (and $\lambda_p$) are Galilean invariant---a
uniform frame translation moves the flow and the wall point together---so the
indicators \eqref{eq:indicators} inherit the locality and frame independence of
the wall-distance dependence already built into the baseline SA model and need
no nonlocal integration. One caveat: where two solid surfaces near the same
point move relative to each other, the ``nearest-wall'' reference for
$|\mathbf{u}|$ can switch discontinuously between them, so $\Gamma$ can be
discontinuous there; the present cases involve no such relative wall motion, and
the general treatment is left to future work.

The three indicators combine into the single nondimensional rate of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:paft},
\begin{equation}
  a(Re_\Omega,\Gamma,\lambda_p) = a_{\max}\,S(z),
  \quad
  z = s(\Gamma-g_c) + \ln\!\Big(1-\big(Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}(\lambda_p)/Re_\Omega\big)^{p}\Big),
  \quad S(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}},
  \label{eq:rate}
\end{equation}
with $a\equiv0$ for $Re_\Omega\le Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}$, and where $S$ is the
logistic function. The sigmoid argument $z$ superposes two effects. The
$\Gamma$ term $s(\Gamma-g_c)$---with $\Gamma$-sigmoid steepness $s\!=\!5.263$ and
center $g_c\!=\!1.572$---switches amplification on as the profile fills out toward
the inflectional value $\Gamma\!\to\!2$; because $g_c$ sits above the
attached-layer asymptote $\Gamma\!=\!1$, it places a Blasius profile on the low
shoulder and inflectional (near-separation) profiles on the high shoulder. The log-barrier term drives
$z\!\to\!-\infty$ as $Re_\Omega\!\to\!Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}$ from above and
vanishes for $Re_\Omega\!\gtrsim\!2\,Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}$ (the high exponent
$p\!=\!4$ makes the cutoff near-Heaviside while leaving the amplifying band away
from the cliff unattenuated). Its baseline cliff is the floor
$Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}\!=\!Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{f}}\!=\!100$, chosen so the Blasius
growth onset matches Drela's $Re_{\theta,\mathrm{crit}}\!\approx\!200$. Because
$Re_\Omega\!=\!d^2\omega/\nu\!\to\!0$
at the wall, the same barrier suppresses the viscous sublayer robustly under mesh
refinement, where $\Gamma\!\to\!1$ is a generic near-wall asymptote rather than a
true instability. The single favorable-pressure-gradient effect enters through the
cliff itself: $Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}$ is a function of $\lambda_p$
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:cliff_fpg} below), rising in accelerating flow so that the
growth-onset threshold is pushed downstream---there is no separate rate-suppression
factor. Both terms depend only on the local mean flow, not on $\tilde\nu$, so the
rate \eqref{eq:rate} preserves the analytic Jacobian of Sec.~\ref{sec:impl}.
Figure~\ref{fig:kernel} maps the kernel.

\paragraph{Favorable-pressure-gradient cliff.} The single
favorable-pressure-gradient mechanism is the cliff floor itself:
$Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}$ rises with $\lambda_p$, pushing the growth-onset
threshold downstream in accelerating flow and matching the airfoil rooftop
start-of-growth location:
\begin{equation}
  Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}(\lambda_p) =
        Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{f}}\,
        \exp\!\big[K_\lambda\,\max(0,\lambda_p)\big].
  \label{eq:cliff_fpg}
\end{equation}
$K_\lambda\!=\!10$ is the study's only scalar fit to the transition cases, set
against the NLF(1)-0416 $\alpha=0^\circ$ rooftop where mfoil's $e^N$ method puts
$N\approx 0$ until $x/c\approx 0.2$.
For $\lambda_p\!\le\!0$ (zero or adverse PG), the cliff floor reverts to
$Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{f}}\!=\!100$ exactly, so Blasius and adverse-PG profiles
are unaffected.

Carrying the favorable-gradient effect entirely through this onset delay follows
Drela's envelope $e^N$ method~\cite{drela_giles_1987}, in which the response to a
favorable gradient is dominated by the critical Reynolds number, not the growth
rate---which lets us anchor the cliff to stability theory rather than treat
$K_\lambda$ as free. Along the Falkner--Skan family the shape factor falls
monotonically as the gradient strengthens ($H\!=\!2.59\!\to\!2.22$ from Blasius to
Hiemenz $\beta\!=\!1$, computed from the self-similar profiles), and the local
indicator tracks it ($\lambda_p\!=\!0\!\to\!1.29$). Over that range Drela's
critical Reynolds number $Re_{\theta c}(H)$ rises steeply---by
$\approx\!27\times$---while his growth rate $d\tilde N/dRe_\theta(H)$ changes only
mildly (to $\approx\!0.7$); the favorable-gradient stabilization is thus almost
entirely an onset delay, exactly what the cliff provides and why no separate rate
factor is needed. In the weak-gradient regime that sets airfoil rooftops the
logarithmic slope $d\ln Re_{\theta c}/d\lambda_p\!\approx\!6.5$ is comparable to
the fitted $K_\lambda\!=\!10$, so the one favorable-gradient constant is consistent
with the $e^N$ critical Reynolds number rather than an independent tuning.

Equation~\eqref{eq:paft} grows $\tilde\nu$ along the flow at the local rate
$a\,\omega$, so $\ln(\tilde\nu/\tilde\nu_\infty)$ accumulates monotonically in the
manner of an amplification factor $N$; because $a_{\max}$ is small
(Sec.~\ref{sec:calib}) the accumulation is gentle and targets the onset
location rather than matching the $e^N$ growth rate quantitatively. Transition is
reached when $\tilde\nu$ has grown to $\chi\sim1$, where the eddy viscosity becomes
dynamically active.

\subsection{Single-equation blend}
A handover weight ties the laminar and turbulent regimes to the single state
variable through $\chi$,
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_t(\chi) = \max\!\left[\,1-e^{-(\chi-1)/\tau},\;0\right] \;=\;
  \begin{cases} 0, & \chi \le 1, \\ 1-e^{-(\chi-1)/\tau}, & \chi > 1, \end{cases}
  \label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}
zero for $\chi\le1$ (laminar) and asymptotically approaching unity as $\chi\!\gg\!1$
(turbulent). The
production and destruction of Eq.~\eqref{eq:sa} are blended,
\begin{equation}
  P = \max\!\big[(1-\sigma_t)\,P_\mathrm{AFT},\;\sigma_t\,P_\mathrm{SA}\big], \qquad
  D = \sigma_t\,D_\mathrm{SA}.
  \label{eq:blend}
\end{equation}
The $\max$ hands the production cleanly from amplification to SA at the
crossover: for $\chi\!\le\!1$ ($\sigma_t\!=\!0$) it returns $P_\mathrm{AFT}$,
leaving the $e^N$ amplification unchanged, and once $\sigma_t P_\mathrm{SA}$
overtakes it just past $\chi\!=\!1$ the source reverts to standard SA with no
laminar residue (a residue, left in, can drive a transition-front limit cycle;
Sec.~\ref{sec:blendnlf}). Gating the destruction by $\sigma_t$ is likewise
essential, since the SA wall-damping destruction would otherwise quench the
gentle amplification; the limit $\sigma_t\!\to\!1$ recovers the unmodified SA
equation and its fully turbulent calibration (log law, freestream decay).
\subsection{Cross-stream diffusion of the laminar disturbance}\label{sec:nulamscale}
The SA diffusion operator is retained in both regimes, but its molecular
coefficient must be reduced for the laminar amplification to survive. We scale
the laminar viscosity in the SA diffusion term---and only there---by a constant
$c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}\!=\!1/12$,
\begin{equation}
  (\nu+\tilde\nu)\;\longrightarrow\;(c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}\,\nu+\tilde\nu)
  \quad\text{in the diffusion term of Eq.~\eqref{eq:sa} only,}
  \label{eq:nulamscale}
\end{equation}
leaving the mean-flow viscosity, the SA production and destruction, and the
definition $\chi=\tilde\nu/\nu$ (formed with the \emph{true} $\nu$) untouched.
Only the molecular coefficient is scaled: relative to it, the nonlinear
self-diffusion $\tilde\nu\,\nabla^2\tilde\nu$ and the $c_{b2}|\nabla\tilde\nu|^2$
gradient term are both smaller by a factor $\mathcal{O}(\chi)$ and hence
negligible where $\chi\!\ll\!1$, so we leave them as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:sa}.

The reduction is needed because, while the disturbance is sub-$\mathcal{O}(1)$
($\chi\!<\!1$), the diffusion coefficient $(\nu+\tilde\nu)$ is dominated by the
molecular part $\nu$, which does not vanish as the working variable does. In a
thin, low-$Re_\theta$ laminar layer the amplifying band is narrow and abuts the
$\tilde\nu\!=\!0$ wall condition, so with the full molecular coefficient the
cross-stream smearing drains the disturbance as fast as the gentle rate
$a\,\omega$ grows it: the Blasius envelope collapses from $N\!\approx\!14$ to
$N\!\approx\!2$ over $Re_x\!\le\!4\!\times\!10^6$ and transition never occurs.
Reducing the molecular coefficient keeps the in-BL $\tilde\nu$ peak co-located
with the $Re_\Omega$ peak and recovers a $dN/dRe_\theta$ in the Drela envelope
range (Fig.~\ref{fig:nuhat}).

This reduction is nonetheless invisible to Spalart's turbulent calibrations. The
constants ($c_{b1},c_{b2},c_{w1},\sigma,\kappa,c_{v1}$) were fixed on
high-Reynolds turbulent flows---free-shear spreading rates and flat-plate skin
friction---and Eq.~\eqref{eq:nulamscale} leaves each intact, by a
region-by-region partition:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Outer layer, wakes, and mixing layers (asymptotic dominance).}
  Wherever these calibrations act, $\tilde\nu\!\gg\!\nu$
  ($\chi\!\gtrsim\!100$), so $c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}\nu+\tilde\nu\!\approx\!\tilde
  \nu\!\approx\!\nu+\tilde\nu$; dividing an already sub-percent molecular
  contribution by twelve is imperceptible, and spreading rates and the outer
  velocity deficit are unchanged.
  \item \emph{Viscous sublayer (exact zero-curvature).} At the wall $\tilde\nu$
  is linear, $\tilde\nu\!\to\!a\,y$; the SA constant
  $c_{w1}\!=\!c_{b1}/\kappa^2+(1+c_{b2})/\sigma$ balances the quadratic
  production, destruction, and self-diffusion so that the \emph{linear} molecular
  diffusion is the surviving near-wall constraint,
  $(c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}\nu/\sigma)\,\partial^2\tilde\nu/\partial y^2\!=\!0$.
  Because $c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}\nu$ is a nonzero constant it still enforces
  $\partial^2\tilde\nu/\partial y^2\!=\!0$: the scaling multiplies a term that
  evaluates to zero and cannot alter the sublayer profile.
  \item \emph{Buffer layer (shielded by $f_{v1}$).} The lone region where
  $\nu\!\sim\!\tilde\nu$ is the buffer layer, and there the change is only a
  $\sim\!15\%$ local reduction in diffusion (at $\chi\!\approx\!5$,
  $(\tfrac{1}{12}+5)/(1+5)\!\approx\!0.85$). Since $\chi$ is formed with the true
  $\nu$, the damping function $f_{v1}\!=\!\chi^3/(\chi^3+c_{v1}^3)$
  ($c_{v1}\!=\!7.1$) still switches on the eddy viscosity at exactly the
  calibrated $\chi$; it absorbs most of the resulting $\tilde\nu$ perturbation
  before it reaches $\nu_t\!=\!\rho\tilde\nu f_{v1}$, the quantity the momentum
  equations feel.
\end{enumerate}
Propagated to the macroscopic solution, the $\sim\!15\%$ buffer-layer deficit
shifts the log-law intercept by a fraction of a percent---of order $0.6\%$ in
skin friction $C_f$. This sits well below the $2$--$5\%$ experimental scatter of
the flat-plate data on which SA was founded, and below typical industrial
discretization error, so Eq.~\eqref{eq:nulamscale} operates entirely within the
sub-percent margin of the original calibration.

\subsection{Assembled single-equation model}
Collecting the three modifications, the SA-AI model solves the single transport
equation
\begin{equation}
  \frac{D\tilde\nu}{Dt} = P - D
  + \frac{1}{\sigma}\nabla\!\cdot\!\big[(c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}\,\nu+\tilde\nu)\,\nabla\tilde\nu\big]
  + \frac{c_{b2}}{\sigma}\,|\nabla\tilde\nu|^2,
  \label{eq:aftsa}
\end{equation}
with the blended production and gated destruction
\begin{equation}
  P = \max\!\big[\,(1-\sigma_t)\,a(Re_\Omega,\Gamma,\lambda_p)\,\omega\,\tilde\nu,\;\;
                 \sigma_t\,c_{b1}\tilde S\,\tilde\nu\,\big],
  \qquad
  D = \sigma_t\,c_{w1}f_w\big(\tilde\nu/d\big)^2,
  \label{eq:aftsa_pd}
\end{equation}
the handover $\sigma_t(\chi)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:sigma} and the amplification rate
$a(Re_\Omega,\Gamma,\lambda_p)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:rate}. It differs from the
baseline SA equation~\eqref{eq:sa} in exactly three places---the amplification
branch of the production $P$, the $\sigma_t$ gate on the destruction $D$, and the
$c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}$ factor on the molecular diffusion---introduces no auxiliary
transport equation, and returns to baseline SA as $\sigma_t\!\to\!1$ (to within
the sub-percent diffusion scaling of Sec.~\ref{sec:nulamscale}).

\section{Kernel constants from canonical stability limits}\label{sec:calib}

The amplification rate \eqref{eq:rate} carries a global magnitude $a_{\max}$, the
$\Gamma$-sigmoid pair $(s,g_c)$, the cliff floor $Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{f}}$, and the
favorable-pressure-gradient onset-delay slope $K_\lambda$.
Rather than fit these to the transition cases that follow, we anchor them to
canonical stability limits---all but the favorable-gradient onset-delay
$K_\lambda$, the single constant fit to a transition case (the NLF(1)-0416
rooftop, Sec.~\ref{sec:nlfval})---so that the cases become predictive tests.

The magnitude $a_{\max}$ is the free-shear amplification ceiling. The sigmoid
saturates ($S\!\to\!1$) as $\Gamma\!\to\!2$, the value a fully inflectional
profile reaches---the detached shear layer over a laminar separation bubble.
There the instability is the inviscid Rayleigh/Kelvin--Helmholtz mode of a free
shear layer, whose maximum growth rate scaled by the local vorticity is a
classical $\mathcal{O}(0.15$--$0.2)$~\cite{michalke_1964}; we identify $a_{\max}$
with this free-shear ceiling and set $a_{\max}\!=\!0.15$.

With the ceiling fixed at $\Gamma\!=\!2$, the pair $(s,g_c)$ sets how far the rate
is throttled at the attached-layer value $\Gamma\!=\!1$---the wall-asymptote
($\omega d/|V|\!\to\!1$) of any attached zero- or favorable-pressure-gradient
layer. Placing $g_c$ between the attached ($\Gamma\!=\!1$) and inflectional
($\Gamma\!=\!2$) limits and choosing the slope $s$ so that the throttled rate
$a(\Gamma\!=\!1)$ reproduces the \emph{Blasius envelope} of the $e^N$
method---Drela's approximate envelope gives $dN/dRe_\theta$ of order $10^{-2}$ for
the zero-pressure-gradient profile~\cite{drela_giles_1987}---fixes
$(s,g_c)\!=\!(5.263,1.572)$. The depth of $\Gamma\!=\!1$ in the sigmoid foot is
thus exactly what encodes the Blasius $e^N$ growth rate, as verified below
(Fig.~\ref{fig:nuhat}).

The $\Gamma$-independent cliff floor $Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{f}}\!=\!100$ places the
Blasius growth onset at Drela's $Re_{\theta,\mathrm{crit}}\!\approx\!200$, through
the $\max_y Re_\Omega\!\approx\!2.2\,Re_\theta$ relation that carries the classical
$\theta$-based onset criterion into the present local variable. The cliff needs no
$\Gamma$-tilt: favorable-pressure-gradient onset delay is handled by $\lambda_p$
through $Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}(\lambda_p)$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:cliff_fpg}), which also
suppresses the near-wall region where $\Gamma\!\to\!1$ but $Re_\Omega\!\to\!0$.

On a Blasius profile the local rate $a\,\omega$ is concentrated in a band at
intermediate wall distance---the part of the profile where Tollmien--Schlichting
waves extract energy from the mean shear---and vanishes in the viscous sublayer
and the outer flow, in the manner of the local growth rate of envelope $e^N$
methods. The $\Gamma$-sigmoid centered at $g_c\!=\!1.572$, above the ZPG
wall-asymptote $\Gamma\!=\!1$, additionally weights the amplification toward the
fuller, more inflectional profiles ($\Gamma\!\to\!2$) that develop under adverse
pressure gradient on airfoils (Secs.~\ref{sec:nlfval}--\ref{sec:eppval}).

To illustrate that the working variable, in its sub-$O(1)$ range, behaves as an
amplification factor, we solve the disturbance transport equation in
non-dimensional boundary-layer variables (length in $\nu/U_\infty$, velocities
normalized by $U_\infty$),
\begin{equation}
  u\,\frac{\partial\hat\nu}{\partial x} + v\,\frac{\partial\hat\nu}{\partial y}
  = \frac{c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}}{\sigma_\mathrm{SA}}\,\frac{\partial^2\hat\nu}{\partial y^2}
    + a(Re_\Omega,\Gamma)\,\omega\,\hat\nu,
  \label{eq:transport}
\end{equation}
using the same SA cross-stream diffusion as the coupled model---coefficient
$\sigma_\mathrm{SA}\!=\!2/3$ with the reduced laminar scale
$c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}\!=\!1/12$ introduced above---on the Blasius layer, seeded
with $\hat\nu_\infty\!=\!1$ at the leading edge as a unit-scale reference, so that
$N\!=\!\ln(\hat\nu/\hat\nu_\infty)\!=\!\ln\hat\nu$ is the amplification factor by
construction. Figure~\ref{fig:nuhat} shows $\ln\hat\nu$ growing monotonically
from the seed, concentrated in the unstable band between $\theta$ and
$\delta_{99}$, and accumulating to $N\!\approx\!14$ over the
$Re_x\!\le\!4\!\times\!10^6$ ($Re_\theta\!\le\!1328$) plate: the working variable
acts as an envelope amplification factor spanning the range required for natural
transition. A physical case seeded with $\chi_\infty$ reaches the handover where
the envelope crosses $N_\mathrm{crit}\!=\!\ln(c_{v1}/\chi_\infty)$---the
half-saturation threshold $\chi\!=\!c_{v1}$ at which the eddy viscosity engages;
the $C_f$ rise begins $\ln c_{v1}\!\approx\!2$ $e$-folds earlier, at
$\chi\!=\!1$. With $\chi_\infty$ taken from Mack's $e^N$ correlation,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:tumap}, the resulting onset matches the Abu-Ghannam \& Shaw
correlation to within $\sim\!8\%$ across the natural-transition range in the
coupled solver (Sec.~\ref{sec:flatplate}).
The reduced laminar diffusion is essential here: with the unmodified coefficient
($c_{\nu,\mathrm{aft}}\!=\!1$) the molecular diffusion of the sub-$O(1)$
$\tilde\nu$ over-damps the gentle amplification and collapses the envelope to
$N\!\approx\!2$, suppressing transition.

The same kernel ($a_{\max}\!=\!0.15$, $(s,g_c)\!=\!(5.263,1.572)$, and the
remaining constants of Table~\ref{tab:const}) is used unchanged for the Blasius
transport illustration above and for every coupled-RANS computation of
Secs.~\ref{sec:flatplate}--\ref{sec:eppval}: no per-geometry adjustment is made.
The favorable-pressure-gradient response---now the onset-delay cliff
$Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}(\lambda_p)$ alone---uses the same $\lambda_p$
pressure-gradient sensor that the Thwaites/Pohlhausen parameter provides for the
Abu-Ghannam \& Shaw onset correlation~\cite{abu_ghannam_shaw_1980} and that the
shape factor $H$ provides for Drela's $e^N$ critical Reynolds
number~\cite{drela_giles_1987}; its slope $K_\lambda$, fit on the NLF rooftop, is
consistent with Drela's $Re_{\theta c}(H)$ through the Falkner--Skan map
$H(\lambda_p)$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:model}), fixing the accelerating-flow stabilization
against stability theory rather than the airfoil data alone.

A single empirical closure remains---the one every $e^N$ method carries---namely
the freestream-turbulence receptivity. A higher $Tu$ is a larger seed
$\chi_\infty$ and thus fewer $e$-folds to the handover, moving transition
upstream, with $N_\mathrm{crit}=\ln(c_{v1}/\chi_\infty)$; we map $Tu$ to the seed
by
\begin{equation}
  N_\mathrm{crit}(Tu) = -8.43 - 2.4\,\ln(Tu_{\mathrm{frac}}),
  \qquad
  \chi_\infty = c_{v1}\,e^{-N_\mathrm{crit}},
  \label{eq:tumap}
\end{equation}
with $Tu_{\mathrm{frac}}$ the rms turbulence intensity as a fraction. This is
\emph{exactly} Mack's~\cite{mack_1977, nasa_tmr} $e^N$ critical-$N$-factor
correlation $N_\mathrm{crit}=A+B\ln Tu$, with $A\!=\!-8.43$ and $B\!=\!2.4$: we
adopt it wholesale rather than fit it. This is legitimate because the SA-AI
seed/threshold structure is isomorphic to the $e^N$ envelope/$N_\mathrm{crit}$
split---the amplification transport plays the role of the (mean-flow-only,
$Tu$-independent) $e^N$ envelope, and $N_\mathrm{crit}=\ln(c_{v1}/\chi_\infty)$
is precisely the $e^N$ threshold, so the seed slope $B$ \emph{is} the Mack
slope. In contrast, the $\gamma$--$Re_\theta$ and BC/BCM
models~\cite{langtry_menter_2009, cakmakcioglu_2018, nasa_tmr} carry their
critical-momentum-thickness closure $Re_{\theta c}(Tu)$ in an auxiliary
transport equation. With the rate structure anchored to stability limits and the
receptivity closure borrowed from Mack, only \emph{one} scalar is fit to the
transition cases: the favorable-gradient onset-delay $K_\lambda$, set on the
NLF(1)-0416 rooftop against the $e^N$ onset (Sec.~\ref{sec:nlfval}). The flat
plate (Sec.~\ref{sec:flatplate}) then \emph{verifies} rather than fits---that the
working variable reproduces the Blasius envelope and that onset falls within the
$e^N$/Abu-Ghannam \& Shaw reference spread---and the adverse-pressure-gradient
Eppler~387 (Sec.~\ref{sec:eppval}) introduces no further constant and is a pure
prediction.

\section{Numerical Implementation}\label{sec:impl}

The model is implemented in Flow360, an implicit, GPU-accelerated, unstructured
finite-volume RANS solver. The only change relative to the baseline SA model is in
the production/destruction of the turbulence equation, Eq.~\eqref{eq:blend}; the
convective and diffusive operators, boundary conditions, and the implicit
pseudo-transient time integration are unchanged. The analytic production Jacobian of
Sec.~\ref{sec:model} is supplied to the same point-implicit linear solve used for the
baseline model. As is standard for SA-like equations, the diagonal contribution of
the production Jacobian is clipped to be non-positive in the implicit assembly
(equivalent to treating the production as a destruction-like term in the linearization
while retaining its full value on the right-hand side); this preserves diagonal
dominance under the gentle amplification, where $\partial P_\mathrm{AFT}/\partial\tilde\nu = a\,\omega > 0$. The freestream boundary value $\tilde\nu_\infty$ is prescribed through
the freestream modified-viscosity ratio $\chi_\infty$; as with all SA computations a
small but nonzero $\chi_\infty$ persists to the body, and here it additionally sets
the disturbance seed. The interior $\tilde\nu$ field is also initialized at this
laminar level $\chi_\infty$, not at the solver's default near-turbulent value: since
the SA dynamics do not relaminarize, a turbulent initial field would otherwise persist
from the leading edge on a fine mesh and bypass the transition model entirely.

\section{Zero pressure gradient: the Blasius flat plate}\label{sec:flatplate}

Figure~\ref{fig:flatplate_batch} computes the model on a zero-pressure-gradient
flat plate with the solver's own SA source terms in a thin-layer boundary-layer
march, at five freestream-turbulence levels spanning the natural-transition
range, $Tu\!=\!0.04\%,\,0.08\%,\,0.16\%,\,0.30\%,\,0.60\%$. The plate is
resolved by a structured $320\!\times\!80$ grid (streamwise $\times$
wall-normal; $25{,}600$ cells). In units of the reference length (unit Reynolds
number $10^6$), the domain spans $0\!\le\!x\!\le\!6$
($Re_x\!\le\!6\!\times\!10^6$) streamwise and $0.5$ in the wall-normal direction;
the streamwise spacing is clustered at the leading edge (from
$\Delta x\!\approx\!8\!\times\!10^{-4}$, coarsening toward the outflow) and the
wall-normal spacing grows geometrically at ratio $1.12$ from a first-cell height
of $\approx\!7\!\times\!10^{-6}$ ($y^+\!\lesssim\!0.3$), resolving the laminar and
transitional boundary layer. The boundary conditions are no-slip on the plate, a
characteristic freestream on the far boundary, and slip walls on the two span
planes (quasi-2D); the freestream Mach number is $0.1$. The
freestream-turbulence receptivity is Mack's $e^N$ correlation
(Sec.~\ref{sec:calib}), adopted without adjustment; the flat plate is a check,
not a fit. We compare the resulting transition onset to the Abu-Ghannam \&
Shaw correlation~\cite{abu_ghannam_shaw_1980} for zero-pressure-gradient
transition onset, $Re_\theta^\mathrm{tr}\!=\!163+\exp(6.91-Tu[\%])$ --- the
community-standard low-disturbance reference, itself a smooth fit through
flat-plate data including Schubauer \& Skramstad~\cite{schubauer_skramstad_1948}.
We use that correlation rather than Schubauer \& Skramstad's raw
Fig.~7 directly because that figure's low-$Tu$ \emph{plateau} (transition
fixed near $Re_x\!\approx\!2.8\!\times\!10^6$ below $Tu\!\approx\!0.08\%$) was
later shown by Wells~\cite{wells_1967} to be largely an artifact of
wind-tunnel acoustic noise rather than a true insensitivity to turbulence ---
a caveat Schubauer \& Skramstad themselves raise.

The Tu $\to\chi_\infty$ map of Eq.~\eqref{eq:tumap} sends each case to a single
freestream seed $\chi_\infty\!=\!c_{v1}\exp(-N_\mathrm{crit})$, with
$N_\mathrm{crit}\!=\!\ln(c_{v1}/\chi_\infty)$ Mack's $e^N$ threshold (map
values $\chi_\infty\!\approx\!2.3\!\times\!10^{-4}$, $1.2\!\times\!10^{-3}$,
$6.3\!\times\!10^{-3}$, $2.9\!\times\!10^{-2}$, $1.5\!\times\!10^{-1}$ at the
five $Tu$). Starting from this small laminar seed, $\tilde\nu$ remains inert
while the skin friction follows the laminar Blasius law (dashed); the
amplification \eqref{eq:paft} grows the disturbance through the unstable region
until $\chi$ reaches unity, after which $C_f$ rises toward the turbulent
log-law correlation (dotted). The standard SA model, by contrast, amplifies its
own production from any nonzero freestream seed and is turbulent from the
leading edge regardless of $\chi_\infty$. As $Tu$ is raised the seed grows, so
fewer $e$-folds are needed to reach $\chi\!\sim\!1$ and the transition moves
forward across the natural-transition range. The same $\hat\nu$ field is
plotted on $(Re_x,Re_y)$ contours next to the velocity field, making visible
the seed-and-amplify pathway.

The two bottom panels collect the streamwise growth of the in-BL maximum
$\chi$ (left, vs $Re_x$) and the resulting $C_f(Re_\theta)$ (right); both draw
black curves with a distinct symbol per $Tu$ and mark the AGS transition-onset
reference with a solid vertical line and a filled symbol on the bottom edge.
The right panel additionally shows the digitized Schubauer--Skramstad Fig.~7
transition band (begin--end, faint horizontal bars) for context. The
operational transition --- the $C_f$ rise --- begins where $\chi$ crosses
unity (the start of the $\sigma_t$ blend) and is essentially complete by
$\chi\!=\!c_{v1}\!=\!7.1$ (the eddy-viscosity half-saturation point); both are
drawn as horizontal references on the bottom-left panel. With the receptivity
closure taken directly from Mack---no constant fit on this plate---the model's
$\chi\!=\!1$ crossing nonetheless reproduces the AGS onset to within $\sim\!7\%$
in $Re_\theta$ through $Tu\!=\!0.30\%$ (best at low $Tu$: $-0.2\%$ at
$Tu\!=\!0.04\%$), rising to $+7.7\%$ at the $Tu\!=\!0.60\%$ edge of the range,
and the onset falls within the Schubauer--Skramstad band throughout. The eddy-viscosity rise from
$\chi\!=\!1$ to $c_{v1}$ is deliberately gentle, so the modeled transition is
more gradual in $Re_\theta$ than the experimental band is wide --- a consequence
of the soft handover \eqref{eq:sigma} chosen for stable implicit coupling. The
seed slope $B\!=\!2.4$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:tumap} is Mack's $e^N$
value~\cite{mack_1977}, adopted rather than fit; growth begins near
$Re_\theta\!\approx\!200$, matching Drela's $Re_{\theta,\mathrm{crit}}$ for
Blasius.

The model is scoped to natural transition. Strongly bypass-dominated
conditions ($Tu\!\gtrsim\!1\%$, e.g. the ERCOFTAC T3A and T3B test cases at
$Tu\!=\!3\%$ and $6\%$) lie outside this scope: the seed
$\chi_\infty$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:tumap} already exceeds $c_{v1}$ at
$Tu\!\gtrsim\!1\%$, which is not a meaningful kinematic parametrisation of
bypass transition. The mechanism in those experiments is streak-induced and
requires a closure that responds to the freestream turbulence energy directly.
We therefore do not present T3A/T3B comparisons here; this is a known scope
limit of the single-equation formulation.

\section{Favorable pressure gradient: NLF(1)-0416}\label{sec:nlfval}

The natural-laminar-flow airfoil NLF(1)-0416 is taken as the primary airfoil test:
the same geometry that demands an $e^N$-style instability tracker to be predicted
correctly. Its extended favorable-gradient rooftop sustains a laminar run over
nearly half the chord at the design incidence, the transition front is genuinely
marginal across a wide $\alpha$ range, and both surfaces transition aft at
$\alpha\!\approx\!0^\circ$---the canonical low-drag-bucket geometry. The model is
evaluated at $Re\!=\!4\!\times\!10^6$, $M\!=\!0.1$,
$\chi_\infty\!=\!c_{v1}\,e^{-9}\!\approx\!8.76\!\times\!10^{-4}$
(matched to $N_\mathrm{crit}\!=\!9$ via the half-saturation anchor
$\chi_\infty\!=\!c_{v1}\,e^{-N_\mathrm{crit}}$: at $\chi\!=\!c_{v1}$ the SA
eddy-viscosity function $f_{v1}(\chi)\!=\!\chi^3/(\chi^3+c_{v1}^3)$ is exactly
$\tfrac12$, so $N_\mathrm{crit}\!=\!\ln(c_{v1}/\chi_\infty)$ is the
amplification at which the eddy viscosity is half-engaged from the laminar
seed; via Mack's map, Eq.~\eqref{eq:tumap}, $N_\mathrm{crit}\!=\!9$ corresponds
to $Tu\!\approx\!0.07\%$, consistent with the low-turbulence tunnel), across
$\alpha\!\in\!\{0^\circ, 4^\circ, 9^\circ,
15^\circ\}$, on a three-level isotropic refinement ladder of both mesh families
(L0--L2 unstructured cavity-mesher and L0--L2 structured O-grid, twenty-four
cases total). The unstructured mesher uses a shared NLF spline contour with
blunt-trailing-edge--aware spacing ($h_\mathrm{TE}$ scales with first-cell
height $h_0$ at every level; the TE ramp ratio $r_\mathrm{TE}$ decays from
$2.0$ at L0 to $1.25$ at L2 so that the local cell size at fixed chord-distance
from the TE also halves); the structured O-grid is generated with the same
contour by Construct2D. Every length scale---surface point count, $h_0$,
growth-ratio offset $(g-1)$, TE step, and TE ramp---refines by a factor of two
per level. The freestream $\chi_\infty$ is compensated for the slowdown factor
$f_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!0.01$ of Sec.~\ref{sec:blendnlf} so the in-domain ratio matches
the half-saturation anchor (input $\chi_\infty^{(in)}\!=\!8.76\!\times\!10^{-6}$;
in-domain $\chi_\infty\!=\!\chi_\infty^{(in)}/f_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!8.76\!\times\!10^{-4}$).
Figures~\ref{fig:nlfmesh_str} and~\ref{fig:nlfmesh_cav} show the two mesh
families at the leading and trailing edges across the three refinement levels.

The unstructured family is deliberately adversarial. Unlike the usual
``unstructured'' airfoil mesh---which grows a \emph{structured} layer of
wall-normal quadrilaterals or prisms through the boundary layer and triangulates
only the isotropic exterior---the cavity mesher is all-triangle everywhere,
including the boundary layer. There the metric is stretched by
$O(10^2\text{--}10^3)\!:\!1$, and the cavity point-insertion uses an anisotropic
distortion metric (with a skewness/normal-deviation objective) to promote
near-right-angled stretched triangles ($\sim\!90^\circ$-$90^\circ$-$0^\circ$)
over flat slivers ($\sim\!180^\circ$-$0^\circ$-$0^\circ$); but this is a
preference, not a guarantee, and the resolved boundary layer carries many
near-degenerate triangles (visible in Fig.~\ref{fig:nlfmesh_cav}). This directly
stresses the model, whose indicators are all \emph{local} and gradient-based
($Re_\Omega\!=\!d^2\omega/\nu$ from the vorticity, together with $\Gamma$ and
$\lambda_p$), so sliver-induced errors in the least-squares gradients feed
straight into the amplification rate. That the cavity and O-grid families
nevertheless predict the same transition---agreeing in drag to within
$\Delta C_d\!\approx\!6\!\times\!10^{-4}$ through $\alpha\!=\!9^\circ$ (rising to
$\approx\!1\!\times\!10^{-3}$ at $\alpha\!=\!15^\circ$) at L2, once the transition
front is fully converged (below)---is therefore a genuine robustness result, not
merely a grid-convergence check.

\subsection{Marginal natural transition and the production blend}\label{sec:blendnlf}

The NLF(1)-0416 exposes a coupling that fixes the form of the production blend
\eqref{eq:blend}. Its long favorable-gradient rooftop keeps the boundary layer near
neutral over an extended chord fraction, so the amplification ignites only where
recovery begins ($\Gamma\!\to\!1$) and the transition location is marginal. On the
fine structured O-grid, a \emph{convex} blend $\sigma_t P_\mathrm{SA}+(1-\sigma_t)
P_\mathrm{AFT}$ then fails to reach a steady state: the transition front breathes with
a $\pm$few-percent drag oscillation at fixed pseudo-time settings. The oscillation is
not numerical---it is invariant to the pseudo-time step---but a genuine feedback. Its
signature is \emph{global}: the surface pressure at the leading edge, far upstream of
transition, oscillates in near-perfect anti-phase with the lift coefficient
($\mathrm{corr}\approx-1$), which a local disturbance could not produce. The loop is
transition $\to$ boundary-layer displacement $\to$ Kutta circulation $\to$ effective
incidence $\to$ the pressure-gradient (shape-factor) sensitivity of the amplification
at the front $\to$ transition. Because the amplification rate depends on $\Gamma$
through a steep activation \eqref{eq:rate}, even the $\mathcal{O}(1\%)$ circulation
breathing is enough to move a marginal front; on coarser or more dissipative meshes
the feedback is damped and the issue does not appear.

The carrier of this receptivity is exactly the term the convex blend retains past
transition: the $(1-\sigma_t)P_\mathrm{AFT}$ bleed for $\chi>1$, which keeps the
\emph{pressure-sensitive} amplification weighted into the early-turbulent region where
the front lives. The $\max$ blend \eqref{eq:blend} removes it---once $\sigma_t
P_\mathrm{SA}$ overtakes amplification just past $\chi=1$, the production is purely the
pressure-\emph{insensitive} SA term---so the front carries no receptivity to the global
pressure field and the computation converges to steady state. With \eqref{eq:blend} the
O-grid NLF(1)-0416 reaches a steady solution at every incidence,
the transition location agrees with the unstructured mesh
and tracks the $e^N$ reference across the incidence range, and the
laminar/amplification calibration is untouched ($\max$ and convex are identical
for $\chi\le1$).

A second, weaker form of the same coupling persists with the $\max$ blend on
the unstructured cavity grid: a slow chi-front breathing whose amplitude
remains finite at long pseudo-times. A small laminar slowdown factor
$f_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!0.01$ multiplying the amplification production
geometrically through a blend variable
$\mathrm{scale}\!=\!f_\mathrm{slow}^{\,1-g_\mathrm{slow}}$ damps this residual
feedback without shifting the converged transition location. The slowdown
gate $g_\mathrm{slow}(\chi)\!=\!1\!-\!\exp\!\big(-(\chi-\chi_\mathrm{slow})/w_\mathrm{slow}\big)$
holds the slowdown FULLY ON throughout $\chi\!\in\![0,\chi_\mathrm{slow}]$
and only relaxes once $\chi$ crosses the eddy-viscosity half-saturation point;
we set $\chi_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!c_{v1}\!=\!7.1$ (the slowdown gate spans the
entire breathing $\chi\!\in\![1,c_{v1}]$ band where the transition-front
receptivity actually lives) with $w_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!4$. With both
modifications --- the $\max$ production blend and the $c_{v1}$-anchored
geometric slowdown --- all twenty-four cases converge to a steady
state with no residual limit cycle.

\section{Adverse pressure gradient: Eppler 387}\label{sec:eppval}

The Eppler 387 low-Reynolds-number airfoil closes the study with the
adverse-pressure-gradient regime, where---with no constant fit to it---the model
is a pure prediction. The McGhee, Walker, and Millard experiment in
the NASA Langley Low-Turbulence Pressure Tunnel \cite{mcghee_1988} tested this
airfoil from $Re\!=\!6\!\times\!10^4$ to $4.6\!\times\!10^5$ at tunnel
turbulence levels between $0.06\%$ and $0.18\%$; the data are the canonical
reference for low-$Re$ transition modeling and supply the comparison condition
used by Coder and Maughmer's AFT-2014, Coder's AFT-2017, the Langtry--Menter
$\gamma$--$Re_\theta$ benchmark, and the Bas--{\c C}akmak{\c c}{\i}o{\u g}lu
algebraic SA-BCM variant
\cite{coder_maughmer_2014, halila_2022, langtry_menter_2009, cakmakcioglu_2018}.
At these conditions the upper surface carries a long laminar-separation bubble
(LSB) that controls the section drag: laminar separation at the shoulder of the
suction peak, an inflectional shear layer in which the rate kernel must
accelerate amplification, turbulent reattachment, and a short turbulent wedge
to the trailing edge. The bubble is the canonical adverse-pressure-gradient
test for a transition model.

The model is evaluated at the de-facto benchmark condition of the
transition-modeling literature: $Re\!=\!2\!\times\!10^5$, $M\!=\!0.1$, across
$\alpha\!\in\!\{0^\circ,2^\circ,5^\circ,7^\circ\}$, with the freestream seed
$\chi_\infty^{(in)}\!=\!8.76\!\times\!10^{-6}$ matched to $N_\mathrm{crit}\!=\!9$
under the same $f_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!0.01$ slowdown used for the NLF (in-domain
$\chi_\infty\!=\!8.76\!\times\!10^{-4}$, the LTPT-class anchor). Three
refinement levels on two independently-generated mesh families
(unstructured cavity, structured O-grid) give twenty-four cases.

Mesh generation reuses the NLF recipe verbatim. The 56-point Eppler 387
contour is read from the NASA TM 4062 Table I (design coordinates with the
trailing edge thickened from $0$ to $0.01\,\mathrm{in}$ blended at
$x/c\!=\!0.95$; $z_\mathrm{TE}\!=\!\pm0.000833\,c$, half the NLF
blunt-thickness), spline-interpolated, resampled at $N\!\in\!\{200,400,800\}$
points with piecewise-cosine clustering at the LE and TE, and fed to both the
in-house unstructured cavity mesher and Construct2D. The blunt-TE
chord-spacing $h_\mathrm{TE}$ halves per refinement level along with every other
length scale, and the TE-corner growth ratio decays from $2.0$ at L0 to
$1.25$ at L2; the far-field is fixed at $100\,c$. The resulting grids range
from $1.6\!\times\!10^4$ nodes (str L0) to $3.8\!\times\!10^5$ nodes (cav L2).
Figures~\ref{fig:eppmesh_str} and~\ref{fig:eppmesh_cav} show the two mesh
families at the leading and trailing edges across the three levels.

Eppler~387 without retuning and leaves the bubble-controlling amplification

\paragraph{Convergence by transition location.} The receding-front laminar
transient described in Sec.~\ref{sec:blendnlf} for the NLF makes the
residual a poor stopping criterion: a run can show a continuity residual
below $10^{-9}$ while the transition front is still moving by $0.05$--$0.10\,c$
per $10^4$ pseudo-steps. We accordingly stop each case by transition-location
stability instead: the run is broken into $5000$-step batches; after each batch
the in-BL $\chi$ field is sampled along both surfaces and the first chord
location where $\max_y\chi\!>\!1$ is extracted as $x_\mathrm{tr}$; the case
is declared converged when $|\Delta x_\mathrm{tr}|\!<\!0.01\,c$ on both
surfaces for three consecutive batches. All twenty-four E387 cases reached
this criterion in $20$--$35\!\times\!10^3$ pseudo-steps (the consec=3
requirement also recovers cleanly from the early leading-edge ``burst''
transient on the L2 meshes, which can hold $x_\mathrm{tr}\!=\!0$ for the first
two batches before the front begins to recede). The same monitor was
retroactively applied to the NLF runs of Sec.~\ref{sec:nlfval}. For the slowest
cases---the high-incidence NLF airfoil on the finest (L2) grids, where under the
$f_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!0.01$ damping the front recedes only marginally per
$10^4$ steps and a fixed budget under-converges---the front is first advanced to
its settled location with the slowdown released ($f_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!1$) and then
re-damped ($f_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!0.01$) to quench the residual breathing; this
two-stage restart reaches the same $|\Delta x_\mathrm{tr}|\!<\!0.01\,c$ steady
state far faster than $f_\mathrm{slow}\!=\!0.01$ alone, and both routes give the
same converged $C_d$ (to $\sim\!10^{-4}$).

\section{Conclusion}

The Spalart--Allmaras working variable can serve as its own transition
indicator. By replacing the production in the laminar range with a local,
$e^N$-like amplification rate and reverting to standard SA near
$\chi\!\sim\!1$, a single transport equation predicts natural transition
without any auxiliary field---occupying a cell left empty by
amplification-factor transport (which transports a separate factor) and by
algebraic SA-transition models (which keep $\tilde\nu$ turbulent and gate it
externally). Because the amplification rate is independent of the working
variable, the modified source keeps an analytic Jacobian and integrates
implicitly at the cost of the baseline model.

The amplification rate is anchored to canonical stability limits---the free-shear
rate ($a_{\max}$), the Blasius $e^N$ envelope ($s,g_c$), the critical Reynolds
number ($Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{f}}$), and the favorable-gradient onset delay (the
cliff $Re_\Omega^{\mathrm{c}}(\lambda_p)$, consistent with Drela's
$Re_{\theta c}(H)$)---and then exercised across three
pressure-gradient regimes of increasing complexity. The freestream-turbulence
closure $\chi_\infty(Tu)$ is Mack's $e^N$ correlation, adopted wholesale rather
than fit. On a zero-pressure-gradient flat plate (Sec.~\ref{sec:flatplate}) this
closure places transition onset within $\sim\!8\%$ of the Abu-Ghannam \& Shaw
correlation over the natural-transition range, verifying the Blasius envelope. On
the NLF(1)-0416 at $Re\!=\!4\!\times\!10^6$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:nlfval}) the one
favorable-gradient onset-delay constant $K_\lambda$ was set so that growth onset
matches the $e^N$ rooftop ignition, and the laminar-to-turbulent handover was
predicted across $\alpha\!\in\!\{0^\circ,4^\circ,9^\circ,15^\circ\}$ on
twenty-four cases (cavity unstructured $+$ structured O-grid $\times$ three
refinement levels $\times$ four incidences). On the Eppler 387 at
$Re\!=\!2\!\times\!10^5$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:eppval}), with no further constant, the
adverse-pressure-gradient regime was predicted in
the laminar-separation-bubble regime; the signed skin friction shows the
recirculation directly and the transition tracks the $e^N$ reference at the
four canonical incidences across another twenty-four cases on the same
two-family ladder. The same unified constants (Table~\ref{tab:const}) were used
throughout. Across both airfoils the L1$\to$L2 transition-location drift is at
or below the $0.01\,c$ stopping tolerance, and the two independently-generated
mesh families agree to within a few percent of chord; this is reported under a
transition-location-based convergence monitor that replaces the (misleading)
residual-only criterion when a slow receding-front transient is present.

Several extensions follow. Quantitative calibration of the rate against
linear-stability and $e^N$ databases beyond the three anchors used here, and a
direct comparison with the algebraic SA-BCM model on community-standard
benchmarks, would establish accuracy beyond the present demonstration.
Extensions to bypass transition (where the streak-induced mechanism requires a
closure that responds to freestream turbulence energy directly, e.g.\ the
ERCOFTAC T3 series), separation-induced, and crossflow transition are natural
next steps.
